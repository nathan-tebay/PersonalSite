Welcome to my personal website!

I've recently realized that this has been long overdue. While I want this to be a useful resource for anyone who happens upon it, I'm doing this for myself. I recently posted the following thoughts on LinkedIn, and it made it clear to me that I needed to improve my documentation. Not the kind you find in a readme file, but the kind of things that get lost to history. Many times I fail to take pictures or lose them along the way, but they will live on here.

This is to be a place for the links I looked up a dozen times over the years. The articles I found interesting, but will likely never find again. A reminder of my motivation, the challenges I overcame, and ultimately the reward for the effort. This .dev site is to be as much about what developed me as what I developed.

I’m building this to “Help Me Remember” and hopefully help others as they find their way. No I'm not suffering Alzheimer's, but I did this song inspiring.

When I'm a crossroads in life, I often fall back on music for inspiration. I can't play, can't sing but there are some artists that are incredibly relatable even when the experiences they are singing about are worlds away.

Last night I had the honor of attending an intimate sit down with Hayes Carll and about a dozen other fans. One of the questions asked was about the origins of "Help Me Remember". It's a song that gives me tingles every time I hear it. Asking myself felt too personal, it's about a person with Alzheimer asking someone to help them remember their life. I got to witness Hayes play that song not 10 feet from me and I had those tingles. This time it was different, more relatable. In talking about the song Hayes said "Sometimes we all need someone to help us remember." It changed the way I thought about it. It still, in a literal sense, reminded me of the struggles my grandparents endured fighting this disease. Hayes' statement, a man that grew up in different world from me in many ways, brought it back to my current struggle.

After 20 years in software, I once again find myself in one of my least favorite places, finding my next job. Job postings and interviewers where I need to make a great first impression take me out of my comfort zone. I've done a lot of great things in my career, but as projects blend together it becomes hard to remember the impact they had or sometimes even that they happened. Most importantly, as time passes some of the most impressive achievements are muddied by details, irrelevant to history, that stick in my mind. I lose track of what made those projects great and dwell on the struggles that shaped me. I've never been one to shout my achievements from the rooftops. Seldom impressed by my own work, it's all part of the job, I find it difficult to convey how all the paths I've taken over the years have made me resilient, adaptable and relevant now. I wish I had someone to help me remember why it all mattered and more importantly why it stills matters. I've always risen to the occasion and grown from the experience, but then it's onto the next thing with little time to reflect. When interviews feel largely transactional, business needs and all, it's hard to convey all the great things that got me as far as I have come and how they will continue to make me better in the future.
